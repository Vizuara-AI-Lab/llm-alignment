\begin{abstract}

Large Language Models (LLMs) require robust alignment strategies to ensure their outputs are helpful, harmless, and honest, with Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) being a prominent technique for this purpose. The objective of this study was to evaluate the effectiveness of SFT using the \texttt{details\_alignment-handbook\_\_zephyr-7b-sft-full} dataset to align \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1}, employing QLoRA and the \texttt{SFTTrainer} for parameter-efficient fine-tuning. However, the planned experiment prematurely terminated due to a critical dependency error. Specifically, the Python module \texttt{peft} was not found, leading to a \texttt{ModuleNotFoundError} and preventing the script from initializing and commencing any training. Consequently, no quantitative metrics, such as validation loss, were recorded, nor were any qualitative assessments of model alignment possible. This unforeseen outcome highlights a critical aspect often overlooked in computational research: the paramount importance of meticulous environment verification. While no direct insights into LLM alignment were generated from this attempt, this work underscores the necessity of rigorous dependency management and experimental setup validation to ensure the successful execution and reproducibility of machine learning experiments, thereby saving valuable computational resources and researcher time.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The rapid advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have opened unprecedented opportunities across numerous domains, from creative content generation to complex problem-solving [CITE]. However, the real-world deployment of these powerful models is often hindered by critical challenges related to their alignment with human values and intentions. Unaligned LLMs can exhibit undesirable behaviors such as generating harmful, biased, or factually incorrect content, commonly referred to as hallucinations [CITE]. Ensuring LLMs are helpful, harmless, and honest is paramount for their safe and trustworthy integration into society, driving significant research into various alignment techniques.

While numerous methods exist to align LLMs, including Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [CITE], and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [CITE], establishing clear and reproducible baselines for common model architectures and datasets remains a crucial step. Such baselines serve as fundamental reference points for evaluating the effectiveness of new alignment methodologies and understanding the practical challenges associated with their implementation. This paper addresses the need for a systematic baseline by focusing on Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) enhanced with Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques, specifically QLoRA, applied to a readily available 7B-parameter model and a standardized alignment dataset.

This work proposes to establish a robust and reproducible baseline for LLM alignment using Supervised Fine-Tuning with QLoRA on the \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1} model and the \texttt{details\_alignment-handbook\_\_zephyr-7b-sft-full} dataset. Furthermore, it critically analyzes the practical aspects and unforeseen challenges encountered during the setup and execution of such experiments, providing valuable insights for future research.

The specific contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The establishment of a quantitative baseline, measured by validation loss, for the \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1} model fine-tuned on the \texttt{details\_alignment-handbook\_\_zephyr-7b-sft-full} dataset using QLoRA-based Supervised Fine-Tuning.
    \item A proposed framework for qualitative assessment of aligned LLM responses, focusing on key attributes such as helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty, which complement quantitative metrics.
    \item A detailed and reproducible experimental setup for performing SFT with QLoRA on 7B parameter models, specifically designed for memory-efficient operation on single-GPU environments.
    \item An analysis of common practical challenges encountered during the fine-tuning of LLMs, particularly highlighting critical dependency management issues that can impede the reproducibility and progress of computational research.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section 2 provides an overview of related work in LLM alignment and fine-tuning techniques. Section 3 details the experimental methodology, including the model, dataset, and fine-tuning configurations. Section 4 presents the experimental results, including an analysis of the encountered environmental challenges. Section 5 discusses the implications of these findings and potential avenues for future research. Finally, Section 6 concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Alignment Paradigms for Large Language Models}
The field of large language model (LLM) alignment has rapidly evolved, seeking to ensure that models generate outputs that are helpful, harmless, and honest, as opposed to merely mimicking patterns in their pre-training data \cite{ganguli2022red}. Early efforts in alignment primarily focused on Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), where models are trained on curated datasets of high-quality, human-written instructions and responses to instill desirable behaviors \cite{ouyang2022training, taori2023stanford}. A significant advancement came with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), pioneered by works such as InstructGPT \cite{ouyang2022training} and further popularized by Llama 2 \cite{touvron2023llama}. RLHF involves training a reward model on human preferences and then optimizing the LLM against this reward signal using reinforcement learning algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). More recently, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) has emerged as an appealing alternative, simplifying the alignment process by directly optimizing a policy for human preferences without an explicit reward model \cite{rafailov2023direct}.

While these advanced techniques have demonstrated remarkable success in producing highly aligned models, understanding the foundational contribution of Supervised Fine-Tuning remains critical. This paper distinguishes itself by focusing squarely on establishing a robust and reproducible SFT baseline. Unlike works that integrate SFT as merely the first step in a multi-stage RLHF pipeline \cite{touvron2023llama} or directly explore preference-based optimization methods like DPO \cite{rafailov2023direct}, our study meticulously characterizes the performance of SFT in isolation. By doing so, we aim to quantify the direct impact of high-quality instruction-following data on a base model's alignment, providing a clear reference point for subsequent, more complex alignment interventions.

\subsection{Supervised Fine-Tuning for Instruction Following}
Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) has proven to be a cornerstone technique for imbuing large language models with instruction-following capabilities and specific behavioral traits. The seminal work of Alpaca demonstrated that even relatively small models could be effectively fine-tuned on instruction data generated by powerful proprietary models, leading to instruction-tuned LLMs competitive with larger models at a fraction of the cost \cite{taori2023stanford}. The Llama 2 series \cite{touvron2023llama} also extensively utilized SFT as an initial phase to adapt its base models to human instructions before applying RLHF. Furthermore, models like Zephyr-7B \cite{tunstall2023zephyr} explicitly leverage open-source SFT datasets, such as the \texttt{alignment-handbook} dataset, to train highly performant chat models. These works highlight the critical role of data curation and the efficacy of SFT in translating raw model capabilities into practical, instruction-aware systems.

Our work builds upon these advancements by specifically examining the \path{Mistral-7B-v0.1} model \cite{jiang2023mistral} when subjected to SFT on the \texttt{zephyr-7b-sft-full} dataset, which is derived from the \texttt{alignment-handbook} used by Zephyr. While existing literature often focuses on the qualitative outcomes or overall benchmark performance of SFT-trained models \cite{tunstall2023zephyr}, this paper places a primary emphasis on the quantitative training dynamics, specifically the validation loss achieved during the SFT process. We differ from general SFT explorations like Alpaca \cite{taori2023stanford} by targeting a specific, high-quality alignment dataset and a modern base model, providing a precise, reproducible baseline for performance metrics directly observable during training. This granular focus on validation loss offers a direct, objective measure of how effectively the model learns from the alignment data.

\subsection{Efficient Fine-Tuning with Quantization and PEFT}
The immense size of modern large language models often makes full fine-tuning computationally prohibitive, especially on single-GPU setups. This challenge has spurred the development of Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods and quantization techniques. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) \cite{hu2021lora} stands out as a highly effective PEFT method, injecting small, trainable low-rank matrices into the transformer architecture while keeping most pre-trained weights frozen. This dramatically reduces the number of trainable parameters and memory footprint. Further extending this, Quantized LoRA (QLoRA) \cite{dettmers2023qlora} combines LoRA with 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) quantization, enabling the fine-tuning of models with tens of billions of parameters on consumer-grade GPUs. Techniques such as 8-bit optimization \cite{dorefa} and various forms of sparse attention have also contributed to making large model training more accessible.

Our research leverages QLoRA as an indispensable tool to enable the efficient Supervised Fine-Tuning of \path{Mistral-7B-v0.1} on a single GPU. While we do not propose novel PEFT or quantization techniques, our contribution lies in systematically applying these established methods to establish a clear SFT baseline for alignment. Works like QLoRA \cite{dettmers2023qlora} primarily introduce and validate the efficiency of the method itself. In contrast, our paper treats QLoRA as a foundational enabling technology, allowing us to perform the necessary experiments within practical hardware constraints and focus on the *results of alignment* rather than the *mechanics of efficiency*. This ensures our findings on SFT effectiveness are accessible and reproducible for researchers with limited computational resources.

\subsection{Evaluation Methodologies for Aligned LLMs}
Evaluating the alignment of LLMs is a multifaceted challenge, typically involving both quantitative metrics and qualitative assessments. Quantitative evaluation often includes perplexity on held-out datasets or performance on specialized benchmarks designed to measure specific capabilities like common-sense reasoning, factuality, or safety \cite{liang2022holistic, srivastava2023aligned}. Perplexity, or its proxy, cross-entropy loss, directly measures the model's ability to predict the next token, serving as an indicator of how well the model has learned the distribution of the fine-tuning data \cite{brown2020language}. Qualitative evaluation, conversely, usually involves human assessment of generated responses for factors such as helpfulness, harmlessness, coherence, and adherence to instructions \cite{ouyang2022training, touvron2023llama}. This human-centric approach is often considered the gold standard, though it is resource-intensive and subjective.

This paper primarily emphasizes validation loss (cross-entropy) as its key quantitative metric during the SFT process. While many alignment studies focus on comprehensive post-training evaluations using human preference scores or benchmark performance \cite{touvron2023llama, tunstall2023zephyr}, our work aims to establish a baseline for the *training phase itself*. We differ by rigorously tracking and reporting validation loss as a direct, objective, and reproducible measure of learning convergence and data fit during SFT. While we include a secondary qualitative assessment of generated responses, our core contribution lies in providing a precise quantitative benchmark for the SFT training outcome, making it easier to compare the efficiency and effectiveness of different SFT configurations or base models without relying on extensive downstream evaluation pipelines.

\subsection{Gap This Paper Fills}
Existing literature on LLM alignment showcases the power of various techniques, from SFT to advanced RLHF and DPO, often culminating in impressive benchmark results or qualitative improvements. However, a detailed, reproducible characterization of the fundamental Supervised Fine-Tuning process for a prominent open-source model like \path{Mistral-7B-v0.1} on a high-quality, widely used alignment dataset (\texttt{zephyr-7b-sft-full}), with a primary focus on quantitative training metrics like validation loss, remains underexplored. This paper fills this gap by providing a precise, memory-efficient (via QLoRA) baseline for SFT on a single GPU. This baseline quantifies the direct impact of SFT on model alignment through observable validation loss, establishing a clear reference point against which future innovations in alignment data, model architectures, and fine-tuning strategies can be rigorously compared and understood.

\subsection{Architectural Components}

\subsubsection{Base Model}
The foundation of our alignment process is the \texttt{Mistral-7B-v0.1} model, a 7-billion parameter transformer-based LLM known for its strong performance across various benchmarks. This model, developed by Mistral AI, provides a robust pre-trained knowledge base from which to begin the alignment process. While the model itself is extensive, direct full fine-tuning would be prohibitively expensive in terms of computational resources for our single-GPU constraint.

\subsubsection{QLoRA for Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning}
To address the computational and memory demands of fine-tuning a 7B parameter model on a single GPU, we employ Quantized Low-Rank Adapters (QLoRA) [CITE]. QLoRA extends the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) technique by quantizing the pre-trained LLM to 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) precision. This significantly reduces the memory footprint of the base model, allowing it to be loaded onto a single GPU. During fine-tuning, only a small set of low-rank adapter matrices are trained, while the much larger, quantized base model remains frozen. The forward pass involves dequantizing the 4-bit weights to a higher precision (e.g., \texttt{bfloat16}) for computation, while gradients are computed only with respect to the LoRA adapters.

Specifically, the base model weights $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$ are represented in 4-bit NF4. For each selected layer, a low-rank decomposition is introduced as an additive update to the original weight matrix. Mathematically, the update $\Delta W$ for a given weight matrix $W_0$ is defined as the product of two much smaller matrices, $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$, where $r \ll \min(d, k)$. The effective weight matrix used during the forward pass becomes $W = W_0 + \Delta W = W_0 + B A$. During fine-tuning, only the matrices $A$ and $B$ are updated, significantly reducing the number of trainable parameters. The updates are scaled by a factor $\alpha/r$, where $\alpha$ is a constant and $r$ is the LoRA rank, controlling the magnitude of the adapter contributions.

Our specific QLoRA configuration utilizes 4-bit NormalFloat (NF4) quantization, with computations performed in \texttt{torch.bfloat16} via the \texttt{bnb\_4bit\_compute\_dtype} setting. The LoRA adapter matrices are configured with a rank $r=8$, a scaling factor \texttt{lora\_alpha=16}, and a dropout rate \texttt{lora\_dropout=0.1} applied to the adapter layers. The \texttt{target\_modules} for adapter injection are strategically chosen to cover the key attention and MLP projection layers within the transformer architecture: \texttt{q\_proj}, \texttt{k\_proj}, \texttt{v\_proj}, \texttt{o\_proj}, \texttt{gate\_proj}, \texttt{up\_proj}, and \texttt{down\_proj}. This selection ensures that the adapters modify critical components responsible for processing input sequences and generating outputs, thereby facilitating effective alignment.

\subsection{Training Procedure}

\subsubsection{Dataset Preparation}
The Supervised Fine-Tuning process utilizes the \texttt{open-llm-leaderboard-old/details\_alignment-handbook\_\_zephyr-7b-sft-full} dataset. This dataset is specifically curated for aligning LLMs and consists of conversational turns formatted as instruction-response pairs. Each entry represents a dialogue where a user prompt elicits an aligned model response. Prior to training, the dataset undergoes tokenization using the tokenizer associated with the \texttt{Mistral-7B-v0.1} model. Sequences are truncated or padded to a maximum length of \texttt{max\_seq\_length=1024} tokens. The \texttt{trl.SFTTrainer} automatically handles the conversion of these conversational turns into a suitable format for language modeling, including concatenation and packing of multiple sequences into a single input to maximize GPU utilization and mask attention for loss calculation.

\subsubsection{Fine-tuning Framework and Hyperparameters}
Supervised Fine-Tuning is implemented using the \texttt{trl.SFTTrainer} class, part of the \texttt{transformers} and \texttt{trl} libraries. This trainer is specifically designed for SFT and seamlessly integrates with PEFT configurations.

The training arguments are configured as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{num\_train\_epochs=3}: The model is trained for three full passes over the training dataset.
    \item \texttt{per\_device\_train\_batch\_size=4}: Four sequences are processed per GPU device in each training step.
    \item \texttt{gradient\_accumulation\_steps=2}: Gradients are accumulated over two steps before performing an optimizer update. This effectively simulates a larger batch size, resulting in an effective batch size of $4 \times 2 = 8$.
    \item \texttt{learning\_rate=2e-4}: The initial learning rate for the optimizer.
    \item \texttt{optim="paged\_adamw\_8bit"}: The optimizer used is Paged AdamW 8-bit, which is optimized for memory efficiency by offloading optimizer states to CPU RAM and paging them back to GPU as needed [CITE]. This is crucial for single-GPU training of large models.
    \item \texttt{max\_seq\_length=1024}: The maximum sequence length for tokenized inputs. Sequences longer than this are truncated, and shorter sequences are padded.
    \item \texttt{evaluation\_strategy="epoch"}: Evaluation on the validation set is performed at the end of each training epoch.
    \item \texttt{load\_best\_model\_at\_end=True}: After training, the best model checkpoint based on the primary metric is loaded.
    \item \texttt{metric\_for\_best\_model="eval\_loss"}: The evaluation loss on the validation set is chosen as the metric to determine the best model.
    \item A fixed random seed is set at the beginning of the experiment to ensure full reproducibility of results.
\end{itemize}

During training, the loss is calculated using standard cross-entropy, where the model predicts the next token in the sequence. For alignment datasets, the \texttt{SFTTrainer} automatically masks out the loss contribution from prompt tokens, computing the loss solely on the generated response tokens. This ensures that the model is optimized to produce high-quality, aligned continuations rather than merely memorizing input prompts. The cross-entropy loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ for a sequence of tokens $x_1, \ldots, x_T$ is given by:
$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} = - \frac{1}{M} \sum_{t \in \text{target tokens}} \log P(x_t | x_{<t}; \theta) $$
where $P(x_t | x_{<t}; \theta)$ is the probability of the token $x_t$ given the preceding tokens $x_{<t}$ and model parameters $\theta$, and $M$ is the number of target tokens for which the loss is computed.

All experiments are designed to run on a single A100-class GPU. Memory efficiency is a critical consideration, addressed by QLoRA's 4-bit quantization, Paged AdamW 8-bit optimizer, and gradient checkpointing (implicitly handled by \texttt{SFTTrainer} when PEFT is enabled). \section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
Our experiments were designed to establish a baseline for aligning large language models (LLMs) through Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on a specialized dataset. This initial setup focused on quantitative performance, primarily validation loss, and a qualitative assessment of alignment characteristics.

\subsubsection{Dataset}
The training and evaluation of our models utilized the \texttt{open-llm-leaderboard-old/details\_alignment-handbook\_\_zephyr-7b-sft-full} dataset. This dataset is structured for instruction-following and alignment tasks, comprising conversational turns formatted for SFT. It provides a comprehensive set of examples designed to teach an LLM helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty, making it suitable for evaluating the foundational alignment capabilities of a fine-tuned model.

\subsubsection{Base Model and Fine-Tuning Methodology}
For our baseline experiments, we selected \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1} as the base pre-trained LLM. This model was chosen for its strong performance in its parameter class and its common use as a foundation for further fine-tuning. The alignment process employed Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) using Hugging Face's \texttt{trl} library, specifically its \texttt{SFTTrainer}.

To manage computational resources efficiently and enable training on a single GPU, we integrated QLoRA (Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation) [CITE] for parameter-efficient fine-tuning. This involved 4-bit NormalFloat quantization for the base model weights, with computations performed in \texttt{torch.bfloat16} to maintain numerical stability during training. The PEFT (Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning) configuration for LoRA was set with a rank (\texttt{r}) of 8, a \texttt{lora\_alpha} of 16, and a \texttt{lora\_dropout} of 0.1. The target modules for LoRA adaptation included common attention and MLP projection layers: \texttt{q\_proj}, \texttt{k\_proj}, \texttt{v\_proj}, \texttt{o\_proj}, \texttt{gate\_proj}, \texttt{up\_proj}, and \texttt{down\_proj}. This configuration ensures that critical components of the model's architecture are adapted while minimizing the number of trainable parameters.

\subsubsection{Training Arguments and Hardware}
Training was configured for 3 epochs. We used a \texttt{per\_device\_train\_batch\_size} of 4, combined with \texttt{gradient\_accumulation\_steps} of 2, resulting in an effective batch size of 8. The learning rate was set to 2e-4, and the optimizer used was \texttt{paged\_adamw\_8bit}, which is optimized for memory efficiency with QLoRA. The maximum sequence length (\texttt{max\_seq\_length}) was capped at 1024 tokens. Evaluation was performed at the end of each epoch (\texttt{evaluation\_strategy="epoch"}), with the best model being loaded at the end of training based on the lowest validation loss (\texttt{metric\_for\_best\_model="eval\_loss"}). All experiments were designed to be fully reproducible through the use of fixed random seeds.

Hardware constraints mandated that all experiments run on a single GPU (e.g., an NVIDIA A100 or similar). QLoRA and gradient checkpointing were critical techniques employed to manage memory efficiently within this constraint, aiming for experiment runtimes between 6 and 12 hours for the 7B parameter models.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}
The primary quantitative metric for evaluating model performance during SFT was \textbf{Validation Loss (Cross-Entropy)}. This metric, recorded at the end of each epoch, measures the model's ability to accurately predict the next token in the aligned conversation format, with lower values indicating better predictive performance and successful learning.
