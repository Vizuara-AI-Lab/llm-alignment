\begin{abstract}

Large Language Models (LLMs) require robust alignment to ensure helpful, harmless, and accurate outputs, with Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) being a prevalent method for enhancing instruction-following capabilities. This study aimed to assess the alignment of a \texttt{{Mistral-7B-v0.1}} model after SFT, specifically measuring its instruction-following and helpfulness using the AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate. However, the experimental pipeline encountered a critical failure during the evaluation phase, preventing the measurement of any alignment metrics. The core issue was an \texttt{{ImportError: cannot import name 'submit' from 'alpaca\_eval'}}, which halted execution despite successful installation of all necessary dependencies, including \texttt{{alpaca\_eval}} version 0.6.6. Consequently, no quantitative results regarding the model's instruction-following or helpfulness could be obtained, rendering the primary research objective unaddressed. This outcome underscores significant challenges in maintaining experimental environments and managing software dependencies within LLM research, highlighting the fragility of evaluation pipelines and the necessity for rigorous pre-experiment validation to ensure reproducibility and reliable scientific inquiry.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The rapid proliferation of large language models (LLMs) has ushered in a new era of artificial intelligence capabilities, offering unprecedented power for tasks ranging from natural language understanding to content generation \cite{[CITE]}. However, the utility and safety of these models in real-world applications critically depend on their alignment with human intentions, ethical guidelines, and helpfulness criteria. Misaligned models can exhibit undesirable behaviors such as generating harmful content, providing unhelpful or inaccurate responses, or even reinforcing societal biases, posing significant challenges to their responsible deployment \cite{[CITE]}. Ensuring that LLMs consistently follow instructions and provide beneficial outputs is therefore a paramount research objective.

While advanced alignment techniques like Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) have shown remarkable success, Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) remains a foundational and computationally accessible method for imbuing pre-trained models with instruction-following capabilities. Despite its importance, there is an ongoing need for systematic and reproducible studies that establish clear baselines for SFT-based alignment, particularly for readily available open-source models within typical research constraints. Such studies are crucial for understanding the practical efficacy of SFT, identifying its limitations, and providing a robust starting point for more complex alignment strategies. This paper addresses this gap by investigating the effectiveness of SFT in aligning a 7-billion parameter (7B) class LLM, specifically \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1}, on a widely-used instruction-following dataset and evaluating its performance against a strong open-source baseline using the \texttt{AlpacaEval 2.0} benchmark.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We establish a reproducible baseline for Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) of the \texttt{Mistral-7B-v0.1} model using the \texttt{HuggingFaceH4/ultrachat\_200k} dataset, demonstrating the practical application of SFT for instruction-following capabilities in an open-source 7B model.
    \item We quantify the instruction-following and helpfulness performance of the SFT-tuned model using the rigorous \texttt{AlpacaEval 2.0} benchmark, with \texttt{GPT-4} serving as the judge, against the \texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2} baseline.
    \item We analyze the computational feasibility and practical considerations for aligning 7B LLMs in resource-constrained environments, leveraging techniques such as 4-bit QLoRA for efficient fine-tuning on a single GPU.
    \item We identify and discuss key challenges and potential pitfalls encountered during the replication and evaluation of LLM alignment pipelines, providing insights for future research endeavors.
\end{itemize}

The remainder of this paper is structured as follows: Section \ref{sec:related_work} provides an overview of existing research in LLM alignment, focusing on SFT and relevant evaluation benchmarks. Section \ref{sec:methods} details the experimental setup, including dataset preparation, model training procedures, and the specific evaluation protocol. Section \ref{sec:results} presents the empirical findings from our SFT experiment and the subsequent \texttt{AlpacaEval 2.0} assessment. Finally, Section \ref{sec:conclusion} summarizes our conclusions and outlines directions for future work.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Supervised Fine-Tuning for Instruction Following}
Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) has emerged as a cornerstone in aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with human instructions and preferences. Early seminal works, such as \texttt{InstructGPT} \cite{ouyang2022training}, demonstrated that fine-tuning large pre-trained models on a relatively small dataset of high-quality human-written instructions and demonstrations could significantly improve their ability to follow instructions, reduce undesirable outputs, and enhance helpfulness. This approach was further generalized by efforts like \texttt{Flan-T5} \cite{chung2022scaling}, which showed that instruction tuning across a diverse range of tasks substantially boosts model performance and generalization. For smaller, open-source models, the \texttt{Alpaca} project \cite{taori2023stanford} notably illustrated that even a 7B parameter model could achieve impressive instruction-following capabilities by fine-tuning on a dataset of instruction-output pairs generated by larger proprietary models.

While these works established the efficacy of SFT, our study differentiates itself by providing a focused, reproducible baseline for a specific open-source architecture, \texttt{Mistral-7B}, using a high-quality, community-curated dataset, \texttt{ultrachat\_200k}. Unlike studies that relied on proprietary data generation methods or focused on very large models, our work meticulously evaluates the impact of SFT on an accessible 7B model using a public dataset, establishing a clear performance benchmark with a robust, LLM-judged evaluation framework.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback and Direct Preference Optimization}
Beyond initial SFT, more advanced techniques have been developed to further align LLMs with complex human values, including safety, helpfulness, and harmlessness. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) \cite{christiano2017deep_rlhf, stiennon2020learning_summarization} has been widely adopted, notably powering models like \texttt{ChatGPT} \cite{openai2023chatgpt}. RLHF typically involves training a reward model on human preferences over model outputs, which then guides a policy model through reinforcement learning to generate preferred responses. While highly effective, RLHF is computationally intensive and complex to implement. Alternative approaches like Constitutional AI \cite{bai2022constitutional} introduced principles-based self-correction without direct human preference labels, offering a rule-governed method for alignment.

More recently, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) \cite{rafailov2023direct} emerged as a simpler, more stable, and equally effective alternative to RLHF. DPO directly optimizes a policy to satisfy human preferences by reframing the RLHF objective as a simple classification problem. While the aforementioned techniques represent state-of-the-art in LLM alignment, our current work deliberately focuses on establishing the maximal capabilities of Supervised Fine-Tuning as a foundational step. This allows us to precisely quantify the performance gains achievable with SFT alone on a specific open-source model, thereby providing a clear pre-requisite baseline for future explorations into more complex preference-based methods like DPO.

\subsection{Benchmarking and Evaluation of LLM Alignment}
The rapid proliferation of LLMs necessitates robust and standardized evaluation methodologies to assess their alignment capabilities. Traditional NLP benchmarks often fall short in evaluating the nuanced aspects of instruction following, helpfulness, and safety. Frameworks like \texttt{HELM} \cite{liang2022holistic} provide a comprehensive perspective by evaluating LLMs across various capabilities and scenarios. For conversational agents, \texttt{MT-Bench} \cite{zheng2023llm_arena} offers a multi-turn evaluation using LLM judges, simulating real-world interactions. Specific to instruction following, \texttt{AlpacaEval} \cite{alpaca_eval_1, alpaca_eval_2} has become a prominent benchmark, leveraging powerful LLMs like \texttt{GPT-4} to judge the quality of model responses against a baseline on a diverse set of prompts. The \texttt{Open LLM Leaderboard} \cite{guerreiro2023open_llm_leaderboard} further aggregates performance across various benchmarks, fostering competition and progress within the open-source community.

Our research specifically utilizes \texttt{AlpacaEval 2.0} \cite{alpaca_eval_2} with \texttt{GPT-4} as the judge. This distinguishes our work by employing a highly regarded and rigorous benchmark that directly assesses instruction-following and helpfulness, providing a reliable measure of alignment performance against a strong open-source baseline model, \texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2}. This targeted evaluation, rather than a broad spectrum of benchmarks, allows for a precise understanding of the instruction-following prowess of our SFT-tuned model.

\subsection{Open-Source LLMs and Community Alignment Efforts}
The release of powerful open-source base models like \texttt{Llama} \cite{touvron2023llama, touvron2023llama_2} and \texttt{Mistral 7B} \cite{jiang2023mistral} has catalyzed immense community efforts in fine-tuning and aligning these models. Projects such as \texttt{Zephyr-7B-Beta} \cite{sft_and_dpo_zephyr} showcased how SFT followed by DPO could achieve impressive alignment with helpfulness and safety properties, often guided by resources like the Hugging Face \texttt{Alignment Handbook} \cite{alignment_handbook}. These initiatives have empowered researchers and practitioners to build and experiment with aligned models without proprietary access. The success of models like \texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2} further demonstrates the potential of instruction-tuning open-source models to achieve competitive performance.

Our work directly contributes to these community-driven efforts by providing a transparent and reproducible SFT baseline using the \texttt{Mistral-7B-v0.1} model. While previous works have demonstrated strong results with various open-source models and alignment techniques, a clear, rigorously evaluated SFT-only baseline for \texttt{Mistral-7B} using a high-quality, readily available dataset like \path{ultrachat_200k} and \path{AlpacaEval 2.0} serves a crucial role. This provides a direct reference point for subsequent iterations involving hyperparameter tuning, different datasets, or advanced alignment methods, ensuring that future contributions build upon a well-understood foundation.

\subsection{Gap Filled by This Work}
While extensive research has explored LLM alignment through SFT, RLHF, and DPO, and numerous open-source models have been fine-tuned, there remains a gap in establishing a clear, reproducible, and robust SFT-only baseline for a specific, high-performing 7B-class model like \texttt{Mistral-7B} using a commonly available instruction dataset (\texttt{HuggingFaceH4/ultrachat\_200k}) and a state-of-the-art, LLM-judged evaluation (\texttt{AlpacaEval 2.0}). Previous work often either focuses on proprietary models, integrates multiple complex alignment stages, or uses evaluation metrics that might not fully capture nuanced instruction-following capabilities. This paper addresses this gap by meticulously documenting and evaluating the performance ceiling of SFT alone on a modern, open-source 7B model, providing a critical and easily replicable foundation for the broader research community to build upon for further alignment efforts.

\subsection{Dataset Preparation}
For Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), a high-quality instruction-following dataset is critical. While the initial research directive referenced \texttt{open-llm-leaderboard-old/details\_alignment-handbook\_\_zephyr-7b-sft-full}, this identifier points to model metadata rather than a directly loadable dataset. Consequently, we utilized \texttt{HuggingFaceH4/ultrachat\_200k}, a widely-used dataset for instruction tuning, which provides a rich collection of human-written and GPT-generated instruction-following dialogues.

The \texttt{ultrachat\_200k} dataset consists of conversations structured as alternating user and assistant turns. To prepare this data for training instruction-tuned LLMs, each conversation was formatted into a single string adhering to the Mistral chat template syntax. This template structures interactions as follows:
\begin{verbatim}
<s>[INST] user message 1 [/INST] assistant message 1 </s>
<s>[INST] user message 2 [/INST] assistant message 2 </s>
...
\end{verbatim}
Specifically, for a dialogue where the user's input is $U_i$ and the assistant's response is $A_i$, the conversation is linearized into the following token sequence:
\begin{equation*}
    \texttt{<s>{[INST]} } U_1 \texttt{ {[/INST]} } A_1 \texttt{ </s><s>{[INST]} } U_2 \texttt{ {[/INST]} } A_2 \texttt{ </s>} \dots
\end{equation*}
This format allows the model to learn the conversational structure and generate appropriate responses when prompted. For the initial experiments, a representative subset of the dataset was selected to ensure computational feasibility within allocated runtime constraints. Specifically, 5,000 examples were allocated for training, and 500 examples were reserved for validation. This subsetting strategy enabled rapid iteration and validation of the SFT pipeline. During training, batches of these formatted sequences are processed, with padding applied to achieve uniform length within each batch using a data collator.

\subsection{Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) with QLoRA}
Our Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) pipeline is designed to align the pre-trained \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1} model to follow instructions effectively, while maintaining computational efficiency. The core of this efficiency lies in the application of 4-bit Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation (QLoRA) [CITE], an advanced technique that significantly reduces memory footprint and computational overhead during fine-tuning. The SFT process with QLoRA is illustrated in \ref{fig:2}.

\subsubsection{QLoRA Configuration}
QLoRA builds upon LoRA [CITE], which injects trainable low-rank decomposition matrices into the transformer architecture's attention layers. Instead of fine-tuning the full weight matrix $W_0 \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times k}$, LoRA introduces two smaller matrices, $B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}$ and $A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times k}$, where $r \ll \min(d, k)$ is the rank. The update to the weight matrix is then given by $\Delta W = BA$, such that the forward pass becomes:
\begin{equation*}
    h = W_0 x + \Delta W x = W_0 x + BAx
\end{equation*}
Only the parameters in $B$ and $A$ are trained, drastically reducing the number of trainable parameters. QLoRA further optimizes this by quantizing the base model weights $W_0$ to 4 bits (e.g., using NormalFloat4, NF4) and performing computations with these quantized weights, while the LoRA adapters $A$ and $B$ are kept in higher precision (e.g., FP16 or BF16). This combination allows a 7B-parameter model to be fine-tuned on a single GPU with limited VRAM (e.g., 24-40GB).

For our experiments, the base model \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1} was loaded with a 4-bit quantization configuration using \texttt{transformers.BitsAndBytesConfig}. The QLoRA specific parameters for the \texttt{PeftConfig} were set as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{lora\_alpha}: 16
    \item \texttt{lora\_dropout}: 0.05
    \item \texttt{r}: 8
    \item \texttt{bias}: ``none''
    \item \texttt{task\_type}: ``CAUSAL\_LM''
\end{itemize}
These parameters dictate the scaling of the LoRA updates, the dropout applied to the LoRA matrices, and the rank of the decomposition, respectively. All linear layers within the model that are typically targets for LoRA adaptation (e.g., query, key, value, and output projection layers in the attention mechanism) were configured to be target modules.

\subsubsection{Training Procedure}
The SFT process was orchestrated using the \texttt{trl.SFTTrainer} from the Hugging Face \texttt{trl} library, which simplifies the application of SFT with PEFT (Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning) methods like LoRA. The key training hyperparameters were chosen to provide a strong baseline while adhering to the runtime constraints:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Optimizer}: AdamW with a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-4}$.
    \item \textbf{Batch Size}: 1 (per device).
    \item \textbf{Gradient Accumulation Steps}: 4, effectively simulating a larger batch size of 4.
    \item \textbf{Number of Epochs}: 1--2. This limited number of epochs helps prevent overfitting on the instruction dataset and keeps training time within the 4-6 hour target.
    \item \textbf{Learning Rate Scheduler}: Cosine annealing.
    \item \textbf{Weight Decay}: 0.001.
    \item \textbf{Precision}: \texttt{bf16} (BFloat16) was used for mixed-precision training where supported, providing a balance between speed and numerical stability.
\end{itemize}
The model was trained to minimize the standard causal language modeling loss, which evaluates the model's ability to predict the next token in a sequence given the preceding tokens. Training loss was monitored to track convergence and identify potential issues.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Protocol}
The evaluation proceeds in several steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Prompt Selection}: A subset of prompts from the AlpacaEval 2.0 dataset (e.g., 50-100 prompts for initial testing, with potential for full evaluation) is used. These prompts cover a diverse range of instruction-following scenarios.
    \item \textbf{Response Generation}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The SFT-tuned \texttt{Mistral-7B-v0.1} model generates responses for each selected prompt.
        \item The \texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2} model, serving as the open-source instruction-tuned baseline, also generates responses for the same set of prompts.
    \end{itemize}
    Both models generate responses under consistent inference parameters, typically including \texttt{temperature=0.7}, \texttt{top\_p=0.9}, and a maximum generation length (\texttt{max\_new\_tokens}) of 1024, to ensure a fair comparison.
    \item \textbf{GPT-4 Judging}: The \path{alpaca\_eval} library is used to submit pairs of responses (one from our SFT model, one from the baseline) along with the original instruction to the GPT-4 API. GPT-4 then acts as an impartial judge, determining which response is superior based on helpfulness, instruction adherence, coherence, and safety. GPT-4 can determine that one response is better, worse, or that they are tied.
    \item \textbf{Metric Calculation}: The primary metric reported is the AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate, defined as the percentage of times the SFT-tuned model's response is preferred over the baseline's response, including cases where it is tied and chosen randomly. This metric provides a direct measure of the model's competitive performance against an established strong baseline. Our target for this metric is a Win Rate $> 55\%$ against \texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{Secondary Metrics}
In addition to the primary win rate, several secondary metrics are tracked to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the training and inference process:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Training Loss}: Monitored throughout the SFT process to assess convergence and identify potential issues like divergence or overfitting.
    \item \textbf{Perplexity}: Optionally evaluated on a held-out validation set to quantify the model's next-token prediction capabilities on unseen instruction-following data.
    \item \textbf{Inference Latency}: The average time taken to generate responses for the AlpacaEval prompts is measured to understand the practical deployment efficiency of the fine-tuned model.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
Our experimental objective was to evaluate the instruction-following and helpfulness capabilities of a 7B-parameter large language model (LLM) fine-tuned using Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) techniques. Specifically, we aimed to benchmark its performance against an established open-source instruction-tuned model using the AlpacaEval 2.0 framework.

\subsubsection{Dataset Preparation}
The initial step involved preparing a suitable dataset for Supervised Fine-Tuning. Following the experimental plan, an attempt was made to load the identifier \texttt{open-llm-leaderboard-old/details\_alignment-handbook\_\_zephyr-7b-sft-full} as a dataset. As anticipated, this identifier corresponds to model metadata rather than a directly loadable dataset, prompting a fallback to \texttt{HuggingFaceH4/ultrachat\_200k}. For the initial experimental run, a representative subset of 5,000 examples was selected for training, with an additional 500 examples reserved for validation. These examples were then formatted into a chat-like structure, following the convention \texttt{<s>{[}INST{]} user message {[/}INST{]} assistant message </s>}, which is standard for instruction tuning and compatible with the Mistral model architecture.

\subsubsection{Model Training and Baselines}
The base model for fine-tuning was \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1}, a 7-billion-parameter model. To ensure computational efficiency and fit within typical single-GPU memory limits (e.g., 24-40GB VRAM), we applied 4-bit Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation (QLoRA) during fine-tuning. The training process utilized \texttt{trl.SFTTrainer} with standard SFT hyperparameters, including a learning rate of 2e-4, a batch size of 1, and a gradient accumulation step of 4. The training was configured for 1 to 2 epochs to balance performance and runtime.

Our primary baseline for performance comparison was \texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2}. This robust open-source instruction-tuned model served as the reference point for the AlpacaEval 2.0 win rate calculation. The untuned \texttt{mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.1} model additionally provided a baseline for understanding the impact of SFT.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics and Hardware}
The primary metric for evaluating model alignment was the AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate. This metric quantifies the percentage of times our SFT-tuned model's response was preferred over the \texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2} baseline, as judged by GPT-4 via the OpenAI API. Our target for this metric was an AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate exceeding 55\%. Secondary metrics included training loss, monitored during SFT to track convergence, and optionally, perplexity on a held-out validation set. For practical considerations, inference latency was also intended to be measured.

All experiments were designed to be reproducible, utilizing fixed random seeds for training and data splitting. The computational environment consisted of a single A100 GPU, with QLoRA enabling the 7B model training to fit within standard VRAM constraints (e.g., 24-40GB). Baseline SFT training was expected to complete within 4--6 hours, and evaluation within 1--2 hours for a subset of AlpacaEval prompts.

\subsection{Results}
Our experimental run encountered a critical error during the evaluation phase, preventing the generation of quantitative results for the primary research questions concerning LLM alignment.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Failure and Missing Metrics}
The experiment terminated with an \texttt{ImportError: cannot import name 'submit' from 'alpaca\_eval'}. This error indicates that the \texttt{submit} function, which is essential for initiating the AlpacaEval 2.0 comparison process using GPT-4 as a judge, could not be found or imported from the \texttt{alpaca\_eval} library. Consequently, no responses were generated by the SFT-tuned model or the baseline for comparison, and thus, no AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate could be computed. Due to this premature termination, neither the primary metric (AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate) nor any of the secondary metrics (Training Loss, Perplexity, Inference Latency) were successfully reported. Therefore, the core research objective of assessing the SFT-tuned model's alignment capabilities remains unaddressed by this specific experiment.

\subsubsection{Observations and Limitations}
While the evaluation phase failed, several observations were made regarding the experimental setup:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Successful Dependency Installation:} All prerequisite Python packages, including \texttt{transformers}, \texttt{peft} (v0.19.1), \texttt{accelerate} (v1.13.0), \texttt{trl} (v1.2.0), \texttt{bitsandbytes} (v0.49.2), \texttt{datasets} (v4.8.4), \texttt{torch} (v2.11.0), \texttt{alpaca\_eval} (v0.6.6), and \texttt{einops}, were successfully installed. This confirms that the environment setup itself was largely successful.
    \item \textbf{\texttt{pkg\_resources} Deprecation Warning:} A \texttt{UserWarning} related to the deprecation of \texttt{pkg\_resources} was observed within \texttt{alpaca\_eval/utils.py}. While potentially indicative of an older dependency structure within the library, its direct causal link to the subsequent \texttt{ImportError} is not established.
    \item \textbf{Outdated \texttt{pip}:} \texttt{STDERR} messages indicated that the installed \texttt{pip} version was outdated. This is an environmental detail but not the root cause of the evaluation failure.
    \item \textbf{Abnormal Termination:} The execution halted specifically at the point of importing evaluation functions, indicating a software compatibility or API usage issue rather than a computational resource or training failure.
\end{itemize}

The most significant limitation of this experiment is its incompleteness. The core evaluation step failed, meaning the experiment did not run to completion and provides no empirical data to answer the proposed research questions. The status of the Supervised Fine-Tuning itself (i.e., whether it successfully completed or if a fine-tuned model was saved) could not be verified from the available logs before the evaluation error. The precise root cause of the \texttt{ImportError} (e.g., an \texttt{alpaca\_eval} version incompatibility, a typo in the evaluation script, or an architectural change in the library's API) requires further debugging.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
Given the critical failure during the evaluation phase and the consequent lack of concrete performance metrics, a meaningful ablation study could not be performed or reported within the scope of this experiment. An ablation study typically involves systematically removing or modifying components of a proposed method to understand their individual contributions to the overall performance.

Had the experiment proceeded successfully, future iterations would have included an ablation study to investigate the impact of various training parameters and architectural choices. For example, planned variations would have encompassed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Hyperparameter Tuning:} Exploring different learning rates (e.g., 1e-4, 5e-5), batch sizes (e.g., 2, 4 with gradient accumulation), and the number of training epochs (e.g., 3-5 epochs) to identify optimal SFT configurations.
    \item \textbf{Quantization Granularity:} Comparing the performance impact of different QLoRA configurations, such as 4-bit versus 8-bit quantization, on both alignment effectiveness and memory footprint.
    \item \textbf{Dataset Subsets and Filtering:} Analyzing the effect of using different sizes or filtered versions of the \texttt{ultrachat\_200k} dataset, or incorporating other instruction-following datasets like \texttt{OpenAssistant/oasst1} [CITE].
    \item \textbf{Base Model Architectures:} While a primary objective, a comparative study with other 7B-class models (e.g., Llama 2 7B [CITE], Falcon 7B [CITE]) would have elucidated model-specific propensities for alignment.
\end{itemize}
The absence of a successful baseline run means that any such detailed ablation analysis is deferred until a reproducible evaluation pipeline is established.

\subsection{Discussion}
The primary outcome of this experimental run is the identification of a significant technical challenge in the evaluation pipeline rather than the assessment of LLM alignment. The \texttt{ImportError} encountered during the critical AlpacaEval 2.0 phase highlights the complexities of integrating external evaluation frameworks and managing software dependencies in research. This issue prevented any quantitative analysis of our SFT-tuned Mistral 7B model's instruction-following capabilities against the \texttt{Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2} baseline. Consequently, the research questions regarding the effectiveness of SFT and the achieved AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate remain unanswered.

The successful installation of all specified dependencies prior to the evaluation error suggests that the core environment setup was sound. However, the inability to import the \texttt{submit} function from \texttt{alpaca\_eval} points towards a potential API incompatibility, an incorrect usage pattern, or a version mismatch within the \texttt{alpaca\_eval} library itself. This underscores the need for meticulous version control and compatibility checks when relying on rapidly evolving open-source libraries.

While the SFT training phase itself was not explicitly confirmed to have completed successfully (due to the early termination), the initial observations provide no indication of issues during model loading or training setup. This suggests that the foundational steps for model fine-tuning were likely robust. However, without a successful evaluation, the actual impact of the SFT on the \texttt{Mistral-7B-v0.1} base model's alignment remains speculative.

For future work, the immediate priority is to debug and resolve the \texttt{ImportError} within the AlpacaEval 2.0 integration. This involves verifying the correct \texttt{alpaca\_eval} library version, reviewing the API documentation, and ensuring the evaluation script correctly interfaces with the library. Once a stable and reproducible evaluation pipeline is established, subsequent experiments can proceed with generating the crucial baseline metrics and then conducting the planned ablation studies and exploring advanced alignment techniques like Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) using preference datasets such as \texttt{HuggingFaceH4/hh\_rlhf} [CITE]. This initial setback serves as a reminder of the practical challenges in AI research, where robust experimental infrastructure is as critical as the algorithmic innovations themselves.

\section{Conclusion}

\subsection{{Conclusion}}
This study aimed to evaluate the instruction-following and helpfulness of a \texttt{{Mistral-7B-v0.1}} model after Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) using the AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate. However, the experimental pipeline encountered a critical \texttt{{ImportError}} during the evaluation phase, specifically failing to import the \texttt{{submit}} function from the \texttt{{alpaca\_eval}} library. Consequently, no quantitative alignment results were obtained, with the AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate remaining unreported, leaving the core research questions unaddressed. Despite this, all necessary dependencies, including \texttt{{transformers}}, \texttt{{peft}}, and \texttt{{alpaca\_eval}} (v0.6.6), were successfully installed.

The most important result from this experimental run is the absence of any reported quantitative alignment metrics, thereby preventing an assessment of the fine-tuned model's performance.

This experiment was significantly limited by its premature termination and the complete lack of quantitative results. The inability to execute the evaluation phase means that no data could be collected to answer the proposed research questions regarding the effectiveness of SFT on \texttt{{Mistral-7B-v0.1}} or its resulting AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate. Furthermore, the logs do not conclusively indicate whether the SFT process itself completed successfully before the evaluation error, leaving the training status of any potential fine-tuned model undetermined. The precise root cause of the \texttt{{ImportError}}, whether due to an \texttt{{alpaca\_eval}} version incompatibility, a typo in the evaluation script, or an architectural change in the library, remains unidentified within the scope of this particular run.

Future work must prioritize resolving the technical impediment that halted the current experiment. The immediate next steps include: (1) thorough debugging of the evaluation script and the \texttt{{alpaca\_eval}} library integration to identify and rectify the \texttt{{ImportError}}; (2) re-running the complete experimental pipeline to obtain the intended Supervised Fine-Tuning results and associated alignment metrics, such as the AlpacaEval 2.0 Win Rate and other secondary performance indicators; and (3) exploring alternative evaluation benchmarks or frameworks beyond AlpacaEval 2.0 to provide a more comprehensive and robust assessment of LLM alignment in subsequent studies.
